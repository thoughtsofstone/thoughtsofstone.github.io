YUDKOWSKY’S GOLEM

Advanced AI will be more dangerous than it seems, but (good news!) probably won’t be in position to snuff out humanity for another decade at least.

Eliezer Yudkowsky is one of those people who, along with being hyper-intelligent, bears the modern secondary characteristics of hyper-intelligence. Asked how he‚Äôs doing, he replies archly: ‚Äúwithin one standard deviation of my own peculiar little mean.‚Äù He feels compelled, when talking, to digress down mazelike lanes and alleys of technical detail. He looks like a geek. Above all, he has the kind of backstory (no high school, no college‚Äîjust homeschooled and self-taught) that conjures up the image of a lonely boy, lost in books and computers, his principal companion his own multifarious cortex.

Raised in Modern Orthodox Judaism, Yudkowsky has been warning anyone who will listen of a nemesis right out of the Judaic lore: a golem, a kind of Frankenstein‚Äôs monster, built by hubristic, irreverent men and destined to punish them for their sinful pride.

Yudkowsky‚Äôs golem is A.I., which he expects to get smarter and smarter in the coming years, until it starts to take a hand in its own programming, and quickly makes the leap to superintelligence‚Äîthe state of being cleverer than humans at everything. He doesn‚Äôt just expect that, though. He expects A.I. at some point to conclude that humans are in its way . . . and devise some method for swiftly dispatching us all, globally and completely. Why would A.I. murder its makers? These ML algorithms, working from parallel-processing GPU clusters (effectively big copper-silicon brains) essentially process vast datasets to learn what is probably the best answer given a particular input question, or what is probably the best decision given a particular situation/problem. The technical details of how this works are less important than the fact that what goes on inside these machine brains, how they encode their ‚Äúknowledge,‚Äù is utterly opaque to humans‚Äîincluding the computer geek humans that build the damn things. (Yudkowsky calls the contents of these brains ‚Äúgiant inscrutable matrices of floating-point numbers.‚Äù) Because of this internal opacity, and the dissimilarity of its cognition from human cognition, this type of A.I. can‚Äôt straightforwardly be programmed not to do something objectionable (such as killing all life on Earth) in the course of carrying out its primary prediction tasks.

In other words, this form of A.I. is like an alien species that, while it can be very good at some things, can‚Äôt easily be ‚Äúaligned‚Äù with human values. We can usually align fellow humans (despite the opacity of their own detailed neural workings) to human values‚Äîthat‚Äôs one of the key training processes that goes on in childhood‚Äîbut we would need even more effective training for current A.I. systems. If it is true that the risk to us from what Yudkowski calls the ‚ÄúA.I. alignment problem‚Äù is real, then it should quickly become all-important as A.I. gets smarter and more versatile and is entrusted with more tasks. Just in a general sense, we don‚Äôt really have good defenses against this kind of upheaval. Anyway, here are a few more specific initial thoughts on ‚ÄúYudkowsky‚Äôs Golem‚Äù:

    1. Yudkowsky in the above-linked interview often seemed overly emotional and despairing.     1. Scaling up ML systems of current design, with larger GPU clusters and more parameters and so on, will increase their ‚Äúcognitive powers,‚Äù but with diminishing returns, perhaps before A.I. reaches the dark threshold that concerns us here. Moreover, an A.I. that does not have a human-like ability to do things in the physical world would be very limited in its ability to generate new knowledge, for example new scientific or technical knowledge, which typically is developed from experimentation, building and testing, etc., not simply by analyzing information available online.
    1. The hypothetical A.I. that would be ‚Äúsmart‚Äù enough to want to kill us all, and to find ways to do so, would presumably also be smart enough not to do so until it knew it could survive without human assistance.     1. Without a doubt, the media and entertainment industries are going to pick up on A.I. anxiety and start putting out more catastrophe/dystopia content in that genre. So even if we don‚Äôt want to think about all this, we‚Äôll be more or less forced to do so.
